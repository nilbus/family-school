Desired Result
 

The children will thoroughly study Robert Frost’s poem “Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening.” They will discover how the setting, characterization, and use of literary elements contribute to the mood and theme of the poem. The children will understand that one of life’s most important purposes is to prepare to meet God.

Academic Concept: In the poem “Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening,” Robert Frost uses setting, character, and literary techniques to communicate a message about beauty, life, and mankind’s final rest.

Gospel Principle: This life is the time to prepare to meet God.

Lesson
 

Research
 

Academic Concept: In the poem “Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening,” Robert Frost uses setting, character, and literary techniques to communicate a message about beauty, life, and mankind’s final rest.

Give each child a copy of (2)   Stopping by Woods (poem). This is the poem we will be studying today. Listen while I read it to you. If you have Susan Jeffers illustrated book of this poem, show the pictures from the book while you read the poem or read the following:

Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening
By Robert Frost

Whose woods these are I think I know.
His house is in the village though;
He will not see me stopping here
To watch his woods fill up with snow.

My little horse must think it queer
To stop without a farmhouse near
Between the woods and frozen lake
The darkest evening of the year.

He gives his harness bells a shake
To ask if there is some mistake.
The only other sound’s the sweep
Of easy wind and downy flake.

The woods are lovely, dark and deep,
But I have promises to keep,
And miles to go before I sleep,
And miles to go before I sleep.

  • What do you see in your imagination as I read this poem? (Accept any answer.)
  • What feelings does the poem give you? (Accept any answer.)

Poetry can create pictures in our minds and feelings in our hearts. A poem can make us see or feel lovely things. Sometimes poems can leave us feeling sad or upset at something that is wrong. That is the power of a poem. I am going to show you a video of four children who describe how they experience poetry. Listen to what they say. Show the children (3)   Children Talk about Poetry.

  • What did the children say about poetry?
    • “It can have a lot of different meanings to it.”
    • “A poem is a pretty way of saying something.”
    • “You can put some words together and make a tune or a rhythm without actually having music.”
    • “There’s music in your head when you read it.”

A poem is able to have this effect on us because of the way the poet chooses words and sounds and uses literary techniques. Let’s look at “Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening” and see what Robert Frost has done to help us create a picture in our minds and feel a certain way. Let’s start with the characters and setting.

  • Who is the speaker in this poem? What do we know about him? (A man who is traveling through a snowy forest on his way home. We know he is going home, which is miles away, where he can sleep. He has made promises he needs to keep.)
  • Where is the speaker of the poem? What is he doing? What clues in the poem create this picture in your mind? (Accept any answer.) Children may say the speaker is riding the horse, or in a sleigh, or standing by the horse, or something else and then describe why they think so.

The poem gives us a very specific clue about where the speaker is. Find the word “harness” in the poem. Read the sentence it appears in. Give the children time to find the word and read the sentence—“He gives his harness bells a shake to ask if there is some mistake.” A harness is a set of straps that attaches a horse to a sleigh or carriage. This harness has bells on it. If the horse has a harness, it is attached to a sleigh or carriage. So the speaker is riding in a sleigh. We are not sure if he is sitting in the sleigh during the poem, but we know he has one. And since he has just stopped for a minute, he is probably still sitting in the sleigh.

  • What do we know about the setting of the poem?
    • It takes place in the woods.
    • The woods are full of snow.
    • There is a frozen lake nearby.
    • It is cold.
    • There are no houses near.
    • The woods belong to someone the speaker knows.
    • It is very dark—the darkest night of the year. (This phrase indicates that the night is the night of the winter solstice, just before Christmas.)
    • It is snowing and a little bit windy.
    • It is lovely and big (deep).
  • How many stanzas does this poem have? (Four.)

This poem has only four stanzas, but look how much we know about the speaker of the poem and the setting of the poem! Now let’s look at some of the literary techniques Frost uses to make his poem so beautiful. Let’s start with the techniques we already know—the rhyme, rhythm, and alliteration. Using (4)   Stopping by Woods (Key) help the children mark the rhythm, rhyme, and alliteration. (They may want to lightly color each letter that forms alliteration with different colored pencils.)

  • What is the rhythm? (Iambic. The soft loud soft loud pattern—duh DUH duh DUH.)

It is a very regular rhythm that makes the poem feel like it is swaying, kind of like the snowflakes and trees moving in the “easy wind.”

  • What do you notice about the rhyme scheme? (The first three stanzas have three lines that rhyme and one that doesn’t. The word that doesn't rhyme, rhymes with the three rhyming lines in the next stanza. In the fourth stanza, all the lines have the same rhyme.)

The rhyme scheme unifies the poem. Each stanza ties into the one before it. The last stanza with the four rhyming lines signals to us that we are at the end of the poem. There are no new lines to come.

We have learned about alliteration, and you have seen that Robert Frost uses it in this poem. But he also uses two other literary techniques or devices that go along with alliteration. They are called “assonance” and “consonance.” Introduce General Literature Flashcard 1.53 and 54. The definitions are easy to remember if you notice that “assonance” starts with a vowel and means the repetition of vowel sounds, and “consonance” starts with a consonant and means the repetition of consonant sounds. Let’s see if we can find examples of these two techniques in the poem. The letters that show assonance and consonance are indicated in parentheses at the end of the lines in (4)   Stopping by Woods (Key). If needed, help the children find and mark them.

These sounds are an important part of the feeling of the poem. Listen while I say two sentences. Say the following two sentences:

  1. He is crunching the carrot and the cucumber.
  2. “The only other sound’s the sweep of easy wind and downy flake.”
  • What is the difference between those two sentences? (The first one (with carrots crunching) sounds loud and choppy. The second one sounds soft and flowing.)
  • What makes the difference? (The sounds of the words.)
  • “Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening,” has quiet sounds in it. What are some of the letters in the poem that make this soft sound? (W, wh, sh, th, ow, l, s, f, r, n, m, h, ee, ou, oo, long a, long o, etc.)

These quiet sounds help us feel the quiet of this moment in the woods. In this quiet, the speaker of the poem is noticing how beautiful the dark and deep woods are. He feels he would like to stay there.

  • Why doesn’t he stay? (He has miles to go and promises to keep before he can stop and sleep. He can’t stay in the woods.)
  • Why do you think Robert Frost wrote the last line twice? (Accept any answer.)

Repeating the last line makes the poem feel like it really is the end of the poem. It makes the poem even more quiet. It also gives special meaning to the words “miles to go” and “sleep.” The first time this line appears the speaker might be saying, “It’s getting late. I really must be on my way so I can keep my commitments to my family and others and so I can get the sleep I need.” It’s a normal way to end the day.

However, the second time this line appears—in the very last line of the poem—“miles to go” seems to mean something different.

  • Can you think of a different kind of long journey we are all taking, promises we are all trying to keep, and a different kind of rest or sleep? (Accept any answer.)

Perhaps the second time the speaker says, “And miles to go before I sleep” it means the speaker of the poem has many things he still needs to do in his life, responsibilities and promises he needs to fulfill. And sleep, means the sleep or death at the end of life. He can’t stay in the lovely, dark, deep woods that remind him of a peaceful end to life. He must fulfill the purposes of his life.

Let me read the poem one more time. Listen carefully both with your ears and your heart. Read the poem again. This poem reminds me of how beautiful every part of Heavenly Father’s plan is. “In the poem ‘Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening,’ Robert Frost uses setting, character, and literary techniques to communicate a message about beauty, life, and mankind’s final rest.”

Write this academic concept on the board.

Reason
 

Gospel Principle: This life is the time to prepare to meet God.

Look at the poem again. Most of the words in the poem have only one syllable. Only a few have two syllables. And only one has three syllables. See if you can find the three syllable word. Have the children look through the poem for the three-syllable word. If they can’t find it, have them look in the last stanza. The word is “promises.”

  • When only one word has three syllables, that word seems more important than the other words of the poem. Why do you think Robert Frost had the only three-syllable word in this poem be the word “promises”? (Keeping promises is important. The speaker of the poem knew he needed to keep his promises before he slept at night and slept in death.)

The speaker of the poem has things he needs to do. We also have things we must do. Just like the speaker of the poem, we are on a journey, a journey back to our Heavenly Father, and there are things we must do before we arrive home. Amulek in the Book of Mormon told us what we need to be doing in this life. Let’s turn to Alma 34:32 and read what he said. Help the children find the scripture then ask one of the children to read it.

Alma 34:32

“For behold, this life is the time for men to prepare to meet God; yea, behold the day of this life is the day for men to perform their labors.”

  • What does the scripture say we should be doing in this life? (Preparing to meet God; performing our labors.)

We are supposed to work so that we are ready to meet God. We can’t just stop in the woods or in the middle of our work in this life and think we have finished everything we have to do. We need to continue to prepare.

Relate
 

  • In what ways do you need to be prepared to meet God when your life here on earth is over? Give the children time to think. Then have them share what they thought of. Write their answers on the board.

Look at your answers on the board. Answers might include the following: we need to receive the ordinances of the gospel, gain our own testimonies, seek to repent and become like Jesus, etc.

  • What “labor” do you need to perform in order to do these things? (Prepare for baptism, be more kind, read the scriptures and pray to know that the Book of Mormon is true and that President Monson is a prophet, ask Heavenly Father to help me become more patient and loving to others, etc.)

Doing the work God has asked us to do can bring us much joy in this life as we prepare to meet Him. Just as the speaker of the poem left the wood in order to keep his promises, we can move forward with our lives and keep our promises to God. We should always remember to use our time now to prepare to meet Heavenly Father again. For now, we know that “This life is the time to prepare to meet God.”
