Desired Result
 

The children will learn about the life of Robert Frost and study one of his poems. They will learn that the words of a poem are given from the point of view of a person called “the speaker.” They will see that the speaker of the poem they are studying gives the reader an invitation to join him in two magical moments. The children will understand that prophets are the speakers for the Lord and that they use love and wonderful promises to invite us to live the gospel.

Lesson
 

Research
 

Academic Concept 1: Robert Frost was a great poet and a kind man.

Teach the children about the life of Robert Frost. Place the pictures from (2)   Moments from Robert Frost’s Life in front of the children as you tell them the information below. You may wish to shorten or skip some of the information. 1

San Francisco Bridge

Robert Frost’s father was born in the southern part of the United States, but later he moved north, to New England. When the Civil War started, he moved his family to San Francisco. Perhaps he moved because his Southern roots made him resist being called a Yankee. He was a journalist and edited the city newspaper.

Robert E. Lee

Robert was born on March 26, 1874, while his family lived in San Francisco. He was named Robert Lee Frost, after the great Civil War general Robert E. Lee.

Return to New England

When Robert was 11, his father died. With his sister and mother, Robert returned to New England. They had no money, so they lived with his paternal grandfather (his father’s father).

Bobbin Boy

Robert worked as a bobbin boy in his grandfather’s textile mill. He didn’t like the job at all. His grandfather was mean and strict and made his mother feel responsible for his father’s death.

Cobbler

Finally, Robert’s mother could not live with her father-in-law any longer. She moved to Salem, New Hampshire, and taught school. Robert got a job as a cobbler and nailed heels to boots to help his mother pay the rent.

Grandfather

Robert did very well in the village school in Salem. His grandfather offered to pay the train fare so Robert could continue his studies at Lawrence High School. He graduated three years later, sharing highest honors in his school with Elinor White, the girl he would marry three years later.

Writing Poetry

Frost began writing poetry when he was 16 and in high school. He jotted down words that came to his mind and made them into poems. He once said, “You catch a poem just as it comes.” However, after he graduated from high school, his grandfather insisted that he attend college. Robert enrolled in Dartmouth College but stayed in school only 2 months. Soon all he did was wander through the woods, reading British poetry and writing his own poems. When he was 19 he sent one of his poems to a magazine called The Independent. The magazine paid for and published the poem, and Robert knew he wanted to be a poet.

Teaching School

Writing poetry, however, did not bring in much money. He tried being a reporter for a local newspaper in order to make money. He liked writing, but he didn’t like asking questions about people. He felt he was prying into matters that were none of his business. He then taught school with his mother and sister. It was during this time that he and Elinor were married. Frost didn’t like teaching young children, and he didn’t make enough money teaching to support his family.

Harvard University

The next year, Robert and Elinor had a baby boy. Robert needed to earn more money, so he decided he would become a college teacher. But in order to teach college, he had to graduate from college. So, with the help of his grandfather, he enrolled in Harvard University. But he thought the classes were boring. He got sick. And worst of all, his little son Eliot died. Frost stayed at Harvard for only two years.

Farm in Derry, New Hampshire

Elinor convinced Robert’s grandfather to help them buy a farm. His grandfather was not excited about helping. Finally he agreed to help if Robert would commit to staying on the farm for ten years. Robert made the commitment, and they bought a dairy farm in Derry, New Hampshire. Robert did all the farm work during the day, and at night he wrote poetry. During his years on the farm, he and Elinor had four more children. They eventually had six children. Only two of them lived longer than Robert did. During these years he ran the farm and taught English two days a week at the Pinkerton Academy. But he didn’t sell very many poems.

Moving to England

After the ten years, the Frosts sold the farm and moved to England. They found a little cottage with a thatched roof and Robert began to write in earnest.

First Book

In spite of the many, many rejections he had received from publishers in the United States, Robert sent thirty of his poems to a publisher in London. Within three days he received his answer. They had decided to publish his poems in a book. It was called A Boy’s Will. The next year, they published a second book called North of Boston. Suddenly Frost was famous. People all over England were talking about him and reading his books. Publishers in America agreed to publish his books there. When Robert heard about the American publishers, he told his wife, “My book has gone home; we must go too.” The Frost family moved back to New England.

Honorary Degrees

When he came home, he was instantly famous. People everywhere wanted to read his poetry. His poetry is simple. Frost uses words most people know and he writes about things that are familiar—birds, snow, hired men, and buzz saws. But they are also full of lessons for life. During his life Frost published 15 books of poetry. Forty-four universities—including Dartmouth and Harvard—gave him honorary degrees. Universities give honorary degrees to people who have achieved greatness in some area of work or service. 2

Pulitzer Prize

This medal is called the Pulitzer Prize. It is an award given to people who write and who compose music. Prizes have been awarded since 1917. Only three people have received four Pulitzer prizes. One of them was Robert Frost. 3

Robert Frost was a Good Man

Robert Frost was a good man. Although he could see weaknesses in people, he did not condemn them. He wrote of how to be better, how to make decisions, and the duties of life, but he did so without being critical or cruel. He loved his land and his people. All of this shows in his writing.

Research
 

Academic Concept 2: Poems are told from the point of view of a person called “the speaker.” The speaker of “The Pasture” invites the reader to participate in two magical moments.

Now that we have learned a little about Robert Frost, let’s read one of his poems. Give each child a copy of (3)   The Pasture. Listen while I read this poem aloud. Read the poem.

I’m going out to clean the pasture spring;                
I’ll only stop to rake the leaves away                              
(And wait to watch the water clear, I may):                   
I shan’t be gone long.—You come too.                          
I’m going out to fetch the little calf                               
That’s standing by the mother. It’s so young,                
It totters when she licks it with her tongue.       
I shan’t be gone long.—You come too.  

  • What is this poem about? (Someone is inviting someone else to come with them to see a spring and a new calf.)
  • How does the person giving the invitation feel about clearing the spring and watching the calf? (Excited, happy; he wants to share the experience with someone else.)
  • Who do you think is speaking in the poem? Who is being invited? (Accept any answer.) Why? (Accept any answer. For example, the person speaking could be a child, a teenager, a friend, a parent or grandparent who is inviting someone they love to come with them to see something fun and magical.)

When we read a poem, we might picture or imagine that the poet is speaking the lines of the poem. But this is not always so. We call the unseen person saying the lines in the poem “the speaker” of the poem. The speaker is the person we imagine is saying the words of the poem. In this poem, we could say, “the speaker is a person who finds joy in two specific things and has invited someone to come enjoy them with him.” It is not necessarily Robert Frost who is inviting someone to see the pasture spring. The poet and the speaker are not necessarily the same person. When we talk about who is speaking in a poem, we say “the speaker.” Introduce General Literature Flashcard 1.93.

  • The speaker of this poem is giving an invitation. Would you like to go with this person? Why or why not? (Accept any answer.)

Frost has the speaker of his poem invite us in such a way that we will want to accept the invitation. Robert Frost makes us want to come along through the use of rhyme, rhythm, and alliteration. The sound of the poem is inviting. Let’s look at the poem and see how he does this. First let’s look at the rhyme scheme. The rhyme scheme is the pattern of rhymes in a poem. We mark them with letters of the alphabet. Introduce General Literature Flashcard 1.61 and 66. We mark all the words that rhyme with each other with the same letter. Let’s mark the rhyme scheme of this poem. Using (4)   The Pasture (Key), help the children mark the rhyme scheme.

Now let’s look at the rhythm. The rhythm is the pattern of stressed (loud) and unstressed (soft) syllables. We mark stressed syllables with a slash mark (/) and unstressed syllables with a smiley (u) mark. Let’s mark the rhythm of the poem. Using (4)   The Pasture (Key), help the children mark the rhythm.

  • A final literary element that makes us, the reader, want to accept the invitation is alliteration. You remember that alliteration is the repetition of beginning sounds of words. Can you find examples of alliteration in this poem? (Accept any answer.) Using (4)   The Pasture (Key), help the children find any examples of alliteration they missed.

The rhyme, rhythm, and alliteration all make the invitation sound fun and exciting, maybe even magical. These are moments the reader will not want to miss. “Poems are told from the point of view of a person called ‘the speaker.’ The speaker of ‘The Pasture’ invites the reader to participate in two magical moments.”

Reason
 

Gospel Principle: The prophet is the speaker for the Lord. He invites us to live the gospel.

I am going to show you a picture of another kind of speaker. He is not a speaker for a poet. Show the picture you have found of the current prophet.

  • Who is this person? (The prophet.)
  • Who does he speak for? (Accept any answer.)

The prophet speaks for the Lord and what he says is the same thing the Lord would say. When the prophet speaks, he tells us what the Lord wants us to know. He is sometimes called the “mouthpiece of the Lord.” We can trust that what he tells us is what God wants us to know.

Like the speaker in Robert Frost’s poem “The Pasture,” the prophets are always inviting us to do the things we need to do so that we can live with Heavenly Father and Jesus and our families forever.

Relate
 

I love listening to the prophets speak in general conference. I want to share something I felt invited by the Lord to do as I listened to His prophet speak. Share with your children the invitation from the prophet you have prepared.

In nearly every talk given in general conference we receive an invitation along with a reward for doing what we are asked to do. As we review and listen to the general conference talks, let’s practice listening for the things the Lord’s speaker invites us to do.

Today we have learned that the words of a poem are communicated through a “speaker.” We also learned that “The prophet is the speaker for the Lord. He invites us to live the gospel.”
