Desired Result
 

Children will be introduced to Handel’s Messiah and hear sacred stories told through music—music with and without lyrics. They will know that Heavenly Father approves of beautiful music!

They will also learn that the Lord has taught us the truth—that music can be a powerful influence for good or evil.

Academic Concept: Music, with or without lyrics, can speak to us.

Gospel Principle: We can know how Heavenly Father views music and its power.

Attention Activity
 

Did you know that music without lyrics (words) can still talk to us? Listen to the following recordings and think about what the music is saying. Have the children get comfortable—lay on the floor, cuddle up on the sofa with a blanket, or snuggle in your lap. Be as quiet as a daisy, even after the music stops. Listen inside your head during this quiet time for what the music says to you.

Play a minute or two of the following instrumental pieces one at a time. Quietly show the appropriate General Music Flashcard 1.61, 70, 76, 19 (Rossini, Sinding, Tchaikovsky, & Brahms) and Musical Selections Halfcards 1.9-11 (William Tell Overture, Rustles of Spring, and 1812 Overture) and Musical Selections Halfcard 1.3 (Brahms Lullaby) as the music is playing. When you think they have listened long enough, turn down the volume as the music fades away into silence. After a moment ask the children to describe their thoughts and feelings. You should be prepared to share your own as an example until listening carefully becomes a familiar skill with your children.

(1)   William Tell Overture, Rossini

(2)   Rustles of Spring, Sinding

(3)   1812 Overture, Tchaikovsky

(4)   Lullaby, Brahms

It is pretty amazing how a song can bring to our minds such a variety of images as running horses or a brook gently flowing or a war scene or a baby quietly going to sleep!

Lesson
 

Research
 

Academic Concept: Music, with or without lyrics, can speak to us.

Today we are going to hear a song that begins a very important story. Most of the time storytelling takes words, but music is so powerful that sometimes, all by itself, it can bring a story to our minds in a most beautiful way.

Pass out the choir books. Does this book look like a storybook? It is, and it tells the most beautiful and important stories in the whole world! They are stories about Jesus. Show (5)   George Frideric Handel. A Baroque composer named George Fridric Handel told these scripture stories by writing an oratorio. Repeat the word "oratorio" with the children. An oratorio is a very long musical composition in which instruments and singers tell a sacred story without using costumes or scenery or acting.

There is something special about two of the songs in this storybook. Look at page x in your choir book. Help the children find this page, the table of contents, in the front of the choir book.

  • How many songs did Handel compose for the Messiah. (Fifty-three!)
  • How many are for groups of singers or for a chorus? (Nineteen.)

The rest of the pieces are for one or two singers, except for two.

  • Can you find the two pieces that have no words or singers? (No. 1 and No. 13.)

Only two of the fifty-three pieces were written for no singers at all. They are called instrumental pieces—only written for musical instruments. In the previous activity, you learned that music played only on instruments, with no lyrics, can cause us to think about a story and feel something special. The first piece, or “The Overture,” in Handel’s Messiah is just this kind of piece. Before we talk about the message of the first piece, let’s learn some things about the story of the whole oratorio!

Overview of The Messiah

As you describe the three parts of the Messiah, softly play (6)   Overture, The Messiah, Handel. The Messiah is divided into three parts. Display (7)   Posters of Handel’s Messiah in Three Parts, Part I.

The Messiah, Part I

Israel anticipates the
coming of the Messiah.
Christ is born in
Bethlehem.

Part I tells about the time before Baby Jesus was born, when so many good people were looking forward to the coming of the promised Messiah—or Jesus. It builds to the climax (high point) as the angels sing of His birth!

Display (7)   Posters of Handel’s Messiah in Three Parts, Part 2.

The Messiah, Part II

Jesus Christ suffers in
mortality and is crucified.
However, the Good News will
be spread to the entire world
and the saints will rejoice!

In Part 2, the Jewish people reject the idea of a meek and lowly Messiah. The choruses and solos describe the tragic mortal experiences endured by Jesus at the hands of wicked people that lead to his death on the cross. Finally, this part of the musical story speaks of a time when the world begins to understand the truth about Jesus’ resurrection and Atonement. It tells the story of a humble carpenter of Galilee who becomes the mighty conqueror of the world. The powerful music speaks of a day when missionaries will tell this story to all the nations of the earth! The joy of the believers is expressed in the final chorus—one that you have probably heard—the most thrilling “Hallelujah” chorus! We’ll hear this song in a minute.

Display (7)   Posters of Handel’s Messiah in Three Parts, Part 3.

The Messiah, Part III

We become part of the chorus that
testifies that Jesus Christ’s
personal victory is also
our victory over death and sin.
At His Second Coming,
we will join in singing
praises and thanks to God!

In Part 3, the oratorio talks about how the life and death and resurrection of Jesus Christ matter to us! Handel’s songs tell us that because of Jesus Christ, we will live again after we die and we can repent and return to Heavenly Father. At this point, Handel’s Messiah becomes Our Messiah, and when He returns to the Earth, we, too, can sing “Thanks!” “Worthy is the Lamb!” and “Amen!”

Overture, The Messiah

The overture or first piece of any drama tries to tell a brief version of the whole story. So as you listen to the “Overture” to the Messiah, you will hear anticipation, worry, sorrow, comfort and great joy! Close your eyes and see with your ears the familiar stories of Jesus as you listen to the music. Show children General Music Flashcard 1.3, 34 (Baroque Era and Handel) as well as Musical Selections Halfcard 1.11 (Overture to the Messiah) and read the backs. Play (6)   Overture, The Messiah, Handel and end with a moment of silence.

  • What did you hear in the music? (Accept any answer.)
  • What part of the Messiah story, or the stories of Jesus, did you think about? (Accept any answer.)

Hallelujah Chorus, The Messiah

Next we will listen to the “Hallelujah” chorus, a song with lyrics, from the Messiah. When Handel’s entire two and one-half hour oratorio was first performed in London, England, in 1743, the audience wasn’t too thrilled. But when the “Hallelujah” chorus began, King George II, filled with emotion, stood up! By law everyone had to follow the King’s example, and so they all stood up!

It seems that King George’s reaction was ahead of its time. The Messiah quickly grew in popularity in England and throughout the world, eventually becoming the best-known and most frequently performed choral work in the western world. And the “Hallelujah” chorus is always the highlight!

Open your choir book to page 193, No. 44-Chorus “Hallelujah!” Try to follow along with the words as we listen to this song. The words go by fast; you will be turning pages pretty quickly, and you will probably get lost the first few times you listen to this piece. Don’t worry about that. Just keep trying to find the words you hear and move right along. Sing as you are able. That’s the way everybody learns to read choir music. Show children General Music Flashcard 1.34 (Handel) and Musical Selections Halfcard 1.12 (Hallelujah Chorus) and read the backs. Play (8)   Hallelujah, Messiah, Handel more than once if you can. Help the children, if you can, to follow this difficult but worth-the-effort music!

Wasn’t that glorious! I think Handel really heard the angels singing when he wrote this piece!

As you see, “Music, with or without lyrics, can speak to us.” 

Write this academic concept on the board.

Reason
 

Gospel Principle: We can know how Heavenly Father views music and its power.

After hearing this music, it may surprise you, but when George Frideric Handel was a small boy in the late 1600’s, many (including Handel’s father) believed God disapproved of people who wrote or even enjoyed music. Since music was thought to be a bad influence, children (including young Handel) were encouraged simply to be quiet, work hard, and follow the rules of the church! People were afraid that the feelings music aroused would cause the loss of self-control.

Let’s compare this to the way God actually views music:

In For the Strength of Youth it says, “Some music can carry evil and destructive messages…that [encourage] immorality or [glorify] violence through its lyrics, beat, or intensity” (For the Strength of Youth, 22).

  • Were the people in Handel’s day right to worry about music? (Yes, because evil music can encourage people to do bad things.)

On the other hand, in the preface to our hymnbook it says, “Some of the greatest sermons are preached by the singing of hymns. Hymns move us to repentance and good works” (Hymns of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, First Presidency Preface, ix).

  • Was Handel right in his pursuit of music? (Yes, because good music can teach the gospel, comfort and inspire us to choose the right.)

Relate
 

In For the Strength of Youth, we are reminded that, “Music has a profound effect on your mind [and] spirit” (pg. 22).

  • How can you make sure that the “profound effect” music has on your mind and spirit is for good? (We can learn to listen to our feelings or to the Holy Ghost, ponder the counsel of our parents and leaders, become familiar with beautiful and wholesome music, turn off or go away from music that we feel or think is bad.)
  • As you listen to parts of the Messiah between now and Christmas, how do you think it will impact your testimony, your love for the Savior, and your ability to live the gospel? (Our love for Jesus and our testimonies will grow bigger, the good music will be a good influence, and our ability to live the gospel will grow stronger.)

As you see, “We can know how Heavenly Father views music and its power.”
