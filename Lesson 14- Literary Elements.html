Attention Activity
 

Give each child a copy of (1)   Geometric Design Coloring Page.

I have given you a page with a geometric design. I also have some colored pencils for you. I would like each of you to color your design. There is only one rule for coloring the design. You must use all six of the colors. You can use them in any places on the design you would like, and you can use some colors more than others, but you must use all six colors.

Give the children several minutes to work on the design. Hopefully, they will have time to finish their design. If not, try to give them enough time so that you can see a pattern develop.

Let’s look at how you’ve colored your designs. Each of you lay your design out in front of you (on the floor or table).

  • What do you notice about the designs? (They are all interesting. They are all different even though we all used the same colors.)

I chose these colors to use because they are the same colors we use to mark our books in this literature class. Each color stands for a different literary element. Authors use all of these literary elements, but each one uses them differently, just as each of you used the same colored pencils differently. Today we will learn about some of the literary elements authors use to create a wonderful story.

Research
 

Give younger children (or any other children who might enjoy it) (2)   Gathering Maple Sap Coloring Pages. Let them color the picture as they want during the lesson.

Look at the handout “Our Literature Pens.” We talked briefly about using these pens in one of our foundation lessons. Today we are going to learn more about them as we use them to help us evaluate and understand the chapters we read for today.

Feel free to make each section shorter or to omit it if needed because of time or the age of your children. If you have to leave any elements out, just review what the literary pens handout says about that element.

Setting

We’ll start with setting. We’ve already looked at some of the setting in Little House in the Big Woods.

  • Does anyone remember what color pen we used? (Green.)

Let’s look at the beginning of Chapter 6: “The Two Big Bears.” The second paragraph gives a description of the setting as spring is coming.

As I read it, let’s underline the paragraph with our green pens.

  • What feeling does this description of the setting give you? (It sounds exciting and pretty, sparkly and sunny.)

We just finished a chapter where the girls were getting tired of winter. This paragraph helps us get excited for spring just like Laura and Mary.

During this time Pa goes to town to sell the animal pelts he has captured during the winter. The words Laura Ingalls Wilder uses to describe this day help us understand how the characters feel about what is happening. Let’s look on the next page and underline the words “The nearest town was far away,” and two paragraphs later, “The sun sank out of sight, the woods grew dark, and he did not come.”

  • What information and feeling do these setting words give you? (Pa has a long way to go and he is walking home all alone. It is dark, and Pa still hasn’t come. The words make you feel afraid. You wonder why Pa isn’t home and hope he isn’t hurt, especially since he didn’t take his gun.)

Later that night, after the girls are in bed and Ma is mending, the setting is described like this: “All around the house the wind went crying as though it were lost in the dark and the cold. The wind sounded frightened.” Let’s underline those words.

  • How do you think Laura and Mary and Ma are feeling now? (Even more scared.)

Everyone seems to be acting calmly, doing ordinary things, but the words of the setting show how frightened they really are.

The next morning Pa is home. Just before he tells “The Story of Pa and the Bear in the Way,” the setting is described in these words: “All that day the sun shone, the snow melted, and little streams of water ran from the icicles, which all the time grew thinner. Before the sun set that night, the bear tracks were only shapeless marks in the wet, soft snow.” Let’s underline those words with our green setting pen (pencil).

  • How do these words help us understand what happened to Pa and how the family feels? (The sun shining and icicles melting takes away the fear and cold of the night before. The bear tracks being only shapeless marks in the snow show that Pa was never in serious trouble.)

Plot

Look at “Our Literature Pens” worksheet.

  • What color is plot? (Pink.)
  • What is plot? (The main events in the story; what happens, why it happens, and the consequences of what happens.)

Let’s use our pink pens and underline the main events of the two chapters we read for today’s lesson.

  • What events should we underline? What do you think are the most important events? The children may say many different events, but they should include the following:
    • Pa goes to town to trade furs. He doesn’t take his gun.
    • Pa doesn’t come home before nighttime.
    • Laura goes with Ma to milk the cow. She carries the lantern.
    • Ma slaps a bear, thinking it’s the cow. She hurries back to the house.
    • Pa comes home and tells the story of his day.
    • A late snow arrives.
    • Pa hurries off to his Pa’s house.
    • Pa comes home in time for dinner. He brings maple sugar and maple cakes with him.
    • Pa tells the story of his day.

Plot also includes why things happen and what the consequences of the actions are.

These are some “why” questions that are answered in the plot. Let’s answer these questions based on the plot.

  • Why did Pa go to town without his gun? (He had too many furs to carry.)
  • Why was Pa so late getting home? (It was hard to walk in the snow on the way to town, and he had to wait for those who arrived before him to finish their trading.)
  • What were the consequences of Pa not having his gun? (He couldn’t shoot the “bear.”)
  • What did Pa do to fight the bear? (He ran at it and made loud noises. Then he hit it with a log.)

Plot helps us see that our decisions matter. Our character and our choices affect our life. A good book shows how the things that happen (or at least the people’s reaction to the things that happen) depend on the character of the people who are involved.

  • Can you think of anything that happened that involved character and decisions? (Answers may vary, but one answer is Laura’s decision to follow her mother exactly without asking why, when they met the bear at the barn.)

Character

  • What color does our handout say we use for character? (Blue.)

Character is what the person looks like physically, as well as what their personality or character is like. It includes what the characters feel, how they react to situations, how they make decisions, and what motivates them.

Let’s see what we learn about the character of Laura, Ma, and Pa from these chapters. We usually record a person’s character on a character chart like the example you have from your foundation lesson. It looks like this. This is a simple example, but it shows us the format for a character chart. First we list information about the external qualities of the character. External qualities are things like their position (king, mother, athlete, etc.) and what they physically look like. You can see from the example that you quote external information from the book, and put the page number at the end.

Then we list internal qualities. Internal qualities are the elements of a person’s character or personality. These qualities could include things like determined, kind, selfish, courageous, shy, bold, etc.—any word we would use to describe a person’s character.

Look at the example. For internal qualities, we first list the quality—helpful, for example. Underneath that word we copy lines from the book that show that the person has that quality, then put the page number in parenthesis. You may have many lines for one quality.

Let’s look at Laura’s character qualities as they are shown in the chapters we read for today.

  • What are some things Laura did in these chapters? (She was proud that her ma asked her to help with the milking when her pa was gone. She did exactly what her ma told her to do when they found the bear. She said her prayers before she went to bed.)
  • What character traits do these actions show? Write the answers on the board. (She wanted to do adult things. She was obedient. She was prayerful.)
  • How could we put those qualities in a character chart? (Let’s make one together and see how to do it.)

Write the following on the board as you explain the process.

First we’ll put “Wanted to Do Adult Things.” While we make this character chart on the board, you can find the places in your book and underline them with your blue pens.

As you write the evidences for this trait on the board, show the children the page where this evidence is found. Do the same with all the lines you copy on the board, so the children can see that you are getting this information from the book and so they can underline the lines in their books.

Then we’ll come down to the next line and write: “Laura was proud to be helping Ma with the milking” (p. 103). Your page number may be different.

Now we’ll do the next character trait. We’ll write on the next line “Obedient.” Then we’ll come down a line and copy the lines from the book that show that Laura was obedient. “Ma said, ‘Laura, walk back to the house.’ So Laura turned around and began to walk toward the house” (pp. 105-106).

We have another line to support this trait. We’ll write this line below the first one. “You were a good girl, Laura, to do exactly as I told you, and to do it quickly without asking why” (p. 106).

Finally, we have the character trait of “Prayerful.” Here’s the line for this trait. “They knelt down by the trundle bed and said their prayers” (p. 115).

We have other lines for this trait, too. “Then there were prayers to be said” (p. 130).

We have learned some important things about Laura’s character in just these few lines.

Style

  • What color of pen do we use for style? (Purple.)

With our purple pens we underline the techniques the author uses that make the writing the “Excellent Craft” that we talked about in our foundation lesson. Style can involve the kinds of words the author uses, or descriptions, or how the author creates character, etc. One of the unique things about of Laura Ingalls Wilder’s style is how she tells much of what happens through Pa’s stories.

  • In the chapters we read for today Pa tells two stories. Do you remember what they are? (The one about meeting the bear that wasn’t a bear, while he was walking home from town, and the one about what a sugar snow is.)
  • Why do you think she has Pa tell this information in stories? (Accept any answer.)

The author could have told the stories herself. She could have said something like this: “While Ma, Laura, and Mary waited at home, Pa walked into town. The snow was slushy and the furs were heavy, so it took him longer to get there than he thought it would. Then he had to wait for other men who had come earlier to have their chance to trade.”

The story could go on like that. Then the book could say, “Pa told the family all about his adventures when he got home.”

  • How is it different having Pa tell the stories instead of the writer?
    • Having Pa tell the stories shows the family doing something together.
    • It shows the love they have for each other.
    • It shows a family tradition they have of telling stories.
    • It shows Pa’s ability to laugh at himself.
    • The other story shows Pa teaching his children about maple sugar and their grandfather.
    • Having Pa tell the stories reinforces the ideas of family and goodness and parents who care.

Laura Ingalls Wilder’s style helps us understand the story and the themes better. Let’s underline the names of the two stories with our purple pens.

Theme

  • What color of pen do we use for theme? (Red.)

Theme is one of the most important aspects of a book. Theme tells us what the author believes. Authors present their beliefs through the stories they write. An example of a theme might be “Real power comes through love, not force.”

You’ll notice that the theme isn’t always written out in a sentence. Much of the theme comes through paying close attention to the other literary elements.

  • As you look at all of the elements we’ve discussed so far—setting, plot, character, and style—what ideas can you think of that might be themes? (Children should be obedient. Parents can have strong families when they teach, discipline, work with, and have fun with their children, etc.)

It can be hard to underline theme. Sometimes authors make a statement that states the theme directly. Then we can just underline the sentence. Sometimes there will be a sentence that doesn’t state the theme directly but helps us think of a theme. We can underline those sentences and write the theme in the margin. Many times, though, the author infers, or suggests, the theme. Sometimes we learn theme through an event that happens. So we could draw a box in red around the paragraphs that reveal a theme, then write on the side what the theme is.

We will become more familiar with theme as we spend more time reading and studying together.

Vocabulary

Our orange pen is for vocabulary. Vocabulary isn’t really a literary element, but paying close attention to vocabulary, or the words we don’t completely understand, can help us get the most out of the books we read. Whenever we see a word we don’t know or understand, we can underline it with our orange pens, look it up, and write the meaning in the margin with our orange pens.

When we started our lesson today, we had already read the two chapters we studied today.

  • Do you feel that you understand these chapters better now? (Yes.)
  • How can studying a book, using the elements of literature help us? (Discuss the answers below.)
    • It can help us better understand what we read.
    • It can help us find and understand the themes.
    • It can help us think about our own lives, our characters, and the themes in the stories that make up our own lives.
