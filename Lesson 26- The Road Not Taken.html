Desired Result
 

The children will read two poems that will help them understand that they can choose their attitudes and the direction of their lives. They will see that as disciples of Christ, they will often make choices that are very different from the choices other people make.

Academic Concept: In the poems “A Minor Bird” and “The Road Not Taken” Robert Frost shows that we choose our attitude and our direction in life.

Gospel Principle: As disciples of Christ we will often need to stand alone and choose what no one else seems to be choosing.

Attention Activity
 

We are going to start today’s literature lesson with a music lesson. I know that seems a little unusual for a literature lesson, but you’ll see why very soon. I am going to show you a video about two different kinds of chords a person can play on the piano. A chord is three or more musical notes that are played at the same time. Listen to the chords. Show the children (1)   Major and Minor Chords.

  • What two kinds of chords did we hear and see in the video? (Major chords and minor chords.)
  • What was the difference? (The major chord sounded brighter and happier. The minor chord sounded subdued or sad.)

In music, a minor chord is a chord that sounds sad. The word minor has another meaning.

  • If I say that we are having a minor problem today, is it a big important problem or a little problem that is not very important? (A little problem that is not very important.)

Something that is “minor” is “something that is not very important.” Now you understand two meanings of the word “minor.” In order to understand one of the poems we will study today we have to understand both definitions.

Lesson
 

Research
 

Academic Concept: In the poems “A Minor Bird” and “The Road Not Taken” Robert Frost shows that we choose our attitude and our direction in life.

A Minor Bird
Give each child a copy of (2)   A Minor Bird.

  • What is the title of this poem? (A Minor Bird.)

As I read this poem, remember the definitions of minor and a minor chord. Read the following poem.

I have wished a bird would fly away,
And not sing by my house all day;

Have clapped my hands at him from the door
When it seemed as if I could bear no more.

The fault must partly have been in me.
The bird was not to blame for his key.

And of course there must be something wrong
In wanting to silence any song. 1

  • What is happening in this poem? (The speaker of the poem can hear a bird singing outside his window. He is annoyed and claps his hands to scare the bird away. He realizes that he is at fault for being annoyed at the bird. The bird’s song was not the problem. The speaker realizes it is not right to silence a bird’s song.)
  • Why do you think Robert Frost called this poem “A Minor Bird”? Think of the two meanings of the word “minor.”
    • Perhaps the bird was singing in a minor key, like a minor chord.
    • The speaker of the poem understands that the song of the bird was actually a minor or small, unimportant problem.
    • The speaker may think the bird is small or unimportant.
  • How did the speaker feel about the bird at the end of the poem? (He wonders if it is his fault for being bothered by the bird’s song. He began to feel that even a minor bird has the right to sing its song.)

Let’s take a minute to look at and mark the rhythm and rhyme scheme of this poem. Have the children mark the rhythm and rhyme scheme. Use (3)   A Minor Bird (Key) if needed.

  • Look at the rhyme scheme. What is it? (AABBCCDD.)

Lines that rhyme like this are called rhyming couplets. This poem has 4 rhyming couplets. Now look at the rhythm.

  • What kind of rhythm do you see? (There are a lot of iambic feet in the poem, but there is no regular rhythm throughout the poem.)
  • We know Robert Frost can write with a consistent rhythm. Why would he not write rhythmically in this poem? Discuss the following ideas with the children:
    • The choppy rhythm is a little hard to read.
    • It doesn’t sound musical.
    • We would expect a poem about a bird song to be musical, but this one isn’t.
    • It helps us remember that the speaker doesn’t like the bird’s song.
  • By the end of the poem does the speaker change his attitude about the Minor Bird’s song? (Yes.)

This poem reminds us that we decide our attitude. The speaker of the poem decided he didn’t like the song of the bird. But later, when he thought about what he had done, he changed his attitude and realized that he shouldn’t stop any creature from singing. His response to the song was his own choice.

The Road Not Taken
Now let’s look at another poem about choices. This poem is one of Robert Frost’s most famous poems. Most people are at least familiar with it and many have memorized it. Let’s listen to this poem, then we will mark the rhyme scheme and rhythm. Give the children (4)   The Road Not Taken, then show them (5)   The Road Not Taken. When you finish listening to the poem, use (6)   The Road Not Taken (Key) to mark the poem.

  • What do you think this poem is about? (Accept any answer.)

Give the children a copy of the first page of (7)   The Road Not Taken Diagram. This picture will help us learn more from the poem.

  • What color are the woods in this poem? (Yellow.) Color the trees and leaves on the ground of your diagram yellow. Give the children time to color, then give them the word strips from (7)   The Road Not Taken Diagram. They will use the 5 blank word strips in the Gospel Principle section of the lesson.

The speaker of this poem has a choice to make. He must decide which road to take. On these wordstrips are words from the poem that tell about each of the two roads or paths he has to choose between. I will read the poem to you again. As I read, I will stop when we get to a description of the path and you can glue the word strip that matches that description on the correct path. Read the poem, stopping after each underlined section. Glue the word strips to the correct path so that they are parallel to the top of the page (mostly perpendicular to the path) and discuss the questions for each section as outlined below.

 

Two roads diverged in a yellow wood,

Glue this word strip where the road divides.

·         What does a divided road symbolize? (A decision, a choice to be made.)

And sorry I could not travel both

And be one traveler, long I stood

And looked down one as far as I could

Glue this word strip on the right path.

·         What could bending in the undergrowth symbolize? (Not being able to clearly see the results of the choice.)

To where it bent in the undergrowth;

 

Then took the other, as just as fair,

Glue just as fair on the left path.

And having perhaps the better claim,

Glue this word strip on the left path.

Because it was grassy and wanted wear;

Glue this word strip on the left path.

·         How was this path different from the first one? (It was not as worn as the first one.)

Though as for that the passing there

Had worn them really about the same,

Glue this word strip between the two paths.

·         What does this phrase tell you about the two paths? (They are about the same.)

 

And both that morning equally lay

Glue this word strip between the two paths.

In leaves no step had trodden black.

Glue this word strip between the two paths.

·         What does this phrase tell you about the paths? (Almost no one has chosen either path.)

Oh, I kept the first for another day!

Yet knowing how way leads on to way,

I doubted if I should ever come back.

 

Glue this word strip on the left path.

·         Which road did the traveler choose? (The one less traveled.)

·         Which road is “the road not taken?” (The one he did not choose to take.)

I shall be telling this with a sigh

Somewhere ages and ages hence:

Two roads diverged in a wood, and I—

I took the one less traveled by,

And that has made all the difference.

 

  • Look at your paths. The speaker of the poem is standing right where the road divides. How do you think he got there? (Accept any answer.)

The speaker is at this particular fork in the road because he has made other choices in the past that brought him there. His previous path has led to this way—this fork in the road, and the choice he makes now will be a way that leads to other ways. The decisions he made earlier in his life brought him to choices that not very many people get to make.

  • How does the speaker think he will feel when he looks back on this choice? (He will feel a little regret or sadness.)
  • How do you know?
    • He says he will “sigh” when someday he tells the story of making this choice.
      • When he chooses the path, he says he “kept the first for another day,” as if he is hoping he will someday be able to go back and choose the other path and see where it leads.
    • The title of the poem is “The Road Not Taken.” The title shows that the speaker believes that later in his life he will still think about the road he didn’t take.
    • How does the path he chooses affect his life? (He says it makes all the difference. It has a huge effect on his life.)

We don’t find out from the poem how the speaker of the poem really feels when he looks back on his choice. We only know that he believes he will wonder about the other path—the road not taken.

The two poems we studied today both present a person faced with choices. The first speaker chooses his attitude—to give up being annoyed at the bird. The second person chooses a direction—a road less traveled. “In the poems ‘A Minor Bird’ and ‘The Road Not Taken’ Robert Frost shows that we choose our attitude and our direction in life.”

Reason
 

Gospel Principle: As disciples of Christ we will often need to stand alone and choose what no one else seems to be choosing.

  • Were there a lot of people on the path the speaker chose in “The Road Not Taken”? (No, it seemed that almost no one had ever traveled this path.)

Because the speaker chose the “road less traveled by,” he had to walk alone. As members of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints and as disciples of Jesus Christ sometimes we will have to make choices that will require us to walk alone. Listen while I tell you a story about a 9-year-old boy who chose “the road less traveled by.” Read the following.

“My soccer team went to a tournament on a Friday, Saturday, and Sunday. My mom asked me what I was going to do about Sunday’s game. I said I didn’t know what to do. My dad encouraged me to pray, so I prayed about it. That night, the song “Nephi’s Courage” got stuck in my head. I knew Heavenly Father answered my prayers through that song so I would remember to have courage to obey His commandments. On Sunday after church, I knew my team was playing. I was a little sad that I couldn’t help my team, but I knew I was doing the right thing. I am glad that Heavenly Father helped me make the right choice, even though it was hard to make.” 2

  • A soccer team has around 15 players. If the boy’s team had this many members, how many chose to play on Sunday? (14.) How many chose not to play on Sunday? (1.)
  • How many team members chose the same path that the boy in our story chose? (0.)

If this boy wanted to follow Christ and be His disciple, he had to make a choice that no one else was willing to make.

Relate
 

You still have five word strips from the paths we colored. Think of five decisions you have made or might make someday that will require you to stand alone—to follow the road that is less traveled. Write the choices on the slips of paper and glue them on the left path of your diagram. Give the children time to think, write, and glue their word strips. Have them share some of their choices.

If you want to follow Jesus and become like Him, there will be many times in your life when you will need to make a hard choice and stand alone. “As disciples of Christ we will often need to stand alone and choose what no one else seems to be choosing.”
