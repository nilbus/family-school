Desired Result
 

The children will analyze the rhyme schemes, rhythm, and imagery of two of Robert Frost’s poems. They will see how these elements contribute to the feeling of joy in the poems. They will understand that we can experience joy and have fun every day if we take time to notice and appreciate the simple, ordinary things around us.

Academic Concept: Using imagery, meter, and rhyme, Robert Frost recreates two happy moments.

Gospel Principle: We can experience joy and have fun every day if we take time to notice and appreciate the simple, ordinary things around us.

Attention Activity
 

To understand our lesson today we need to learn about two types of rhythms poets often use. I will show you the first rhythm. Listen while I clap my hands. Clap your hands in a soft loud soft loud pattern—duh DUH duh DUH. Now you clap the same rhythm with me. Clap the rhythm several times until they can do it easily. Now listen while I clap the same rhythm and also say words with the rhythm. Clap your hands as you read the following poem. Emphasize the stressed syllables with your voice while you are clapping.

I wan-dered lone-ly as a cloud
That floats on high o'er dale and hill
When, all at once, I saw a crowd
A host of gold-en daf-fo-dils.

This rhythm—with a quiet or unstressed syllable followed by a louder or stressed syllable—is called iambic (eye-am’-bik). Introduce General Literature Flashcard 1.68. Show (1)   Iambic Example This time say the words and clap the rhythm with me. Have the children clap while they say the words.

Now let’s learn the second rhythm. Listen again while I clap my hands. Clap your hands in a soft soft loud soft soft loud pattern—duh duh DUH duh duh DUH. Now you clap the same rhythm with me. Clap the rhythm several times until they can do it easily.

  • What is the difference between this rhythm and the iambic rhythm? (This one has two soft or unstressed syllables before the loud or stressed one.)

Let’s try this rhythm with words. Listen to these common sayings. Clap your hands as you read the following. Emphasize the stressed syllables with your voice while you are clapping.

In the blink of an eye
By the skin of your teeth
Hit the nail on the head
At the drop of a hat

This rhythm—with two quiet or unstressed syllables followed by a louder or stressed syllable—is called anapestic (an-u-pes-tik). Introduce General Literature Flashcard 1.70. Show (2)   Anapestic Example. This time say the words and clap the rhythm with me. Have the children clap while they say the words.

The poems we will study today use these two rhythms or meters.

Lesson
 

Research
 

Academic Concept: Using imagery, meter, and rhyme, Robert Frost recreates two happy moments.

Give each child a copy of (3)   Dust of Snow and (4)   Gathering Leaves. These are the poems we will study.

Dust of Snow

Listen while I read “Dust of Snow.” Read the following.

The way a crow
Shook down on me
The dust of snow
From a hemlock tree

Has given my heart
A change of mood
And saved some part
Of a day I had rued. 1

First, I should tell you that to “rue” something means to be sorrowful about something or to bitterly regret something. Now, let’s mark the rhyme scheme and rhythm of this poem. Using (5)   Dust of Snow (Key) help the children mark the rhyme scheme and then the rhythm of the poem.

  • What is the rhyme scheme of this poem? (ABAB CDCD.)
  • What is the major rhythm of the poem? (Iambic.)

The stanzas of a poem are sections of a poem that have the same number of lines and the same meter and rhyme scheme. One stanza is usually separated from the other stanzas by a line space.

How many stanzas are in this poem? (Two.)

Introduce General Literature Flashcard 1.74.

  • What lines in each stanza are not iambic? (The last lines.) What type of rhythm are they? (The last line of the first stanza has an iambic and an anapest rhythm. The last line of the second stanza has two anapests.) Have the children clap the last lines.

Authors sometimes change rhythm to draw attention to the lines. The last line of the first stanza is important because it is the last line of what happened to the speaker. The last line of the second stanza is important because it shows how the speaker has changed. He used to be unhappy. Now he is happy.

  • What do you picture when I read this poem? (A crow landing on a branch and shaking snow off of it. The snow lands on the speaker of the poem.)
  • What words in the poem help you “see” or imagine what is happening? (Shook, dust of snow, hemlock tree.)

These words create what we call “imagery.” An author creates imagery when he or she uses words that help the reader see, hear, smell, feel, or taste something. Introduce General Literature Flashcard 1.80.

  • How did the speaker of the poem feel at the beginning of the poem? (He felt sorrowful. He regretted something about his day.)
  • How did he feel after the snow fell on him? (His mood changed a little. He felt happier.) Why? (Accept any answer.)
  • How do you feel when you read this poem? (Accept any answer.)

That little “dust” of snow changed his feelings. He felt better after the snow fell on him than he did before. I can imagine him smiling a little as the snow melted down his back. It was just a little thing. Some people might have been annoyed if a little bird dropped snow on them, but for the speaker of the poem, this small action took his mind off himself and made him aware of the wonderful world around him.

Gathering Leaves
Now listen while I read “Gathering Leaves.” Read the following:

Spades take up leaves
No better than spoons,
And bags full of leaves
Are light as balloons.

I make a great noise
Of rustling all day
Like rabbit and deer
Running away.

But the mountains I raise
Elude my embrace,
Flowing over my arms
And into my face.

I may load and unload
Again and again
Till I fill the whole shed,
And what have I then?

Next to nothing for weight,
And since they grew duller
From contact with earth,
Next to nothing for color.

Next to nothing for use.
But a crop is a crop,
And who's to say where
The harvest shall stop?

Let’s mark the rhyme scheme, rhythm, and similes in this poem. Using (6)   Gathering Leaves (Key) help the children mark the rhyme scheme and rhythm of the poem and find the similes.

  • What is the rhyme scheme of each stanza of this poem? (ABCB; the 2nd and 4th lines rhyme.) The children may point out that first stanza is ABAB. This is a variation of the rhyme scheme in the rest of the poem.
  • What is the major rhythm of the poem? (Anapestic with some iambic.)
  • Both these poems have quite a regular rhythm and rhyme scheme. What effect does that have on the feeling of the poems? (It makes them feel bouncy and happy, kind of like a song.)
  • Look at the lines with the rhyming words in the 5th stanza.What do you notice about the rhythm of these lines? (Both lines end in an unstressed syllable. They are the only lines in the poem that end in unstressed syllables.)
  • What effect do the unstressed syllables have? (These lines are not as bouncy as the other ones.)

The rhyming words are “duller” and “color.” Maybe the speaker is feeling a little sad that the leaves he or she has collected aren’t colorful anymore. The speaker is noticing that once the leaves are “harvested” they aren’t as pretty as they were when they first fell to the ground. The rhythm of these words emphasizes the little bit of sadness the speaker may be feeling.

What do you see or imagine in your mind as I read this poem? (Someone trying to pick up leaves, but they are very light. He works and works, putting leaves in bags, but he also makes mountains of leaves and tosses them over his head. He puts bags of leaves in the shed.)

  • Which words help you picture in your mind that he has a hard time gathering the leaves? (“Spades take up leaves no better than spoons.”)
  • Which words help you see that he works hard all day long? (“I make a great noise of rustling all day” and “I may load and unload again and again.”)
  • Which words create the image in your mind that he is having a little fun playing with the leaves as he works? (“The mountains I raise elude my embrace, flowing over my arms and into my face.”)
  • Which words help you picture where he is storing his collection of leaves? (“Till I fill the whole shed.”)
  • What does the speaker of the poem say about the value of the leaves?
    • They don’t weigh much (so they won’t sell for much—crops are often sold by weight).
    • They’re not very pretty.
    • They don’t have much use.
  • Then why do you think he bothers harvesting the leaves? What does he say about it?
    • The leaves are a crop like anything else.
    • Who knows where the harvest will stop.
  • What has the harvester of leaves really harvested?
    • The opportunity to work hard. A good work ethic.
    • A good memory of fun—playing in the leaves.

Usually when people rake leaves, they don’t put them in a shed. They throw them away or spread them in their garden. Perhaps the speaker of this poem keeps them because they remind him of a time of hard work and fun. Today we have seen how, “Using imagery, meter, and rhyme, Robert Frost recreates two happy moments.”

Write this academic concept on the board.

Reason
 

Gospel Principle: We can experience joy and have fun every day if we take time to notice and appreciate the simple, ordinary things around us.

The poems we studied today can help us remember something very important, something we can apply to our own lives. Let’s see if you can discover what that is.

  • When you daydream about having fun or about something that might bring you joy, what kinds of things do you think of? (Accept any answer.)

Some people believe they can only find joy and have fun if they take a big vacation, eat a big treat, go to a theme park, or buy something new that costs a lot of money. These kinds of things can certainly be fun and they can bring us joy, but if we think these are the only ways to have fun and find joy, we have a problem.

  • What do you think the problem is? (We cannot have these kinds of experiences every day. If we think that in order to have fun and experience joy we have to spend money or go far away, we will have lots of days without joy or fun.)

The people in our poems experienced joy and fun.

  • What brought them joy and fun? (A surprise snowfall, playing in the leaves.)
  • Did these things require them to spend a lot of money or travel far away? (No.)

Elder Dallin H. Oaks said, “When I think of happiness or joy in this life, I begin with some experiences that are simple and basic.” Elder Oaks said that he finds joy in seeing “the expression on the face of a one-year-old taking those first steps” or watching “a two-year-old immersed in a soft ice cream cone” or watching “a child loving a puppy or a kitten.” He said that we “can also experience joy in what is simple and basic—in flowers and other growing things, in a sunrise or sunset or other beauties of nature, in wholesome companionship.” 2

Relate
 

Like the people in our Robert Frost poems, Elder Oaks finds joy and happiness in the ordinary everyday moments of life, and we can too! We can have fun and experience joy every day! We just need to keep our eyes open. We need to be a little bit like poets and take time to notice the ordinary, wonderful things around us. And, we need to be creative.

  • If we had a yard covered in leaves what could you do? (Accept any answer. Answers may include: first we would have to notice them, perhaps rake them into a pile, then we could jump in the pile, toss the leaves, press the leaves and make pictures with them, have a leaf battle, throw them in the air, gather the leaves together to put in the garbage or a compost pile for a garden, etc.)

Having some fun and experiencing joy is an important part of life. The Lord said, “Men are, that they might have joy”. 3 The two poems we studied today show us that “We can experience joy and have fun every day if we take time to notice and appreciate the simple ordinary things around us.”
